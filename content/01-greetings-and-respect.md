## Words
- apni = you (formal, for elders and in-laws — USE THIS)
- tumi = you (familiar, for fiancée, friends, younger family)
- tui = you (intimate / very informal — do NOT use with in-laws)
- ami = I
- amra = we
- shey / o = he / she (familiar)
- tini / uni = he / she (formal — use about your in-laws)
- ji = yes (formal acknowledgement)
- hyna / ha = yes
- na = no
- dhonnobad = thank you
- maaf korben = please forgive me / sorry (formal)
- dukkhito = I am sorry / regretful
- nam = name

## Phrases
- Apni kemon achhen? = How are you? (formal)
- Ami bhalo achhi, dhonnobad = I am well, thank you
- Apnar shorir kemon achhe? = How is your health? (formal — very polite to in-laws)
- Bhalo achhe = It is well
- Apnar nam ki? = What is your name?
- Amar nam Dag = My name is Dag
- Apnar shathe dekha hoye bhalo laglo = Nice to meet you (lit. "meeting with you felt good")
- Kheyal rakhben = Take care (formal)
- Bhalo thakben = Stay well (formal farewell)
- Dekha hobe = See you (lit. "meeting will happen")
- Ji, dhonnobad = Yes, thank you
- Maaf korben = Excuse me / pardon me

## Grammar notes

### The three "you"s — and why this matters now
Bangla has three forms of *you*, and choosing the wrong one with your in-laws is the single biggest social mistake to avoid:

- **apni** — formal. Use with: in-laws, elders, anyone you don't know, anyone you respect. **This is your default.**
- **tumi** — familiar. Use with: your fiancée, friends, younger siblings.
- **tui** — intimate. Use with: very close childhood friends, very young children. Never strangers, never elders.

Every verb form changes with the pronoun. The notes you have show pairs like `shono / shunen` and `koro / koren` — the *second* form (-en) is always the apni form. Drill the right column.

### Verb endings cheat sheet (apni form)
- Simple present: stem + **-en** (koren = you do)
- Present continuous: stem + **-chhen** (korchhen = you are doing)
- Past: stem + **-len** (korlen = you did)
- Future: stem + **-ben** (korben = you will do)
- Imperative (request): stem + **-en** (koren = please do)

## Practice prompts
- Greet your fiancée's parents in your head every morning on the train
- Practise "Apni kemon achhen?" → "Apnar shorir kemon achhe?" as a two-step opening
- Say goodbye three different ways: kheyal rakhben / bhalo thakben / dekha hobe
