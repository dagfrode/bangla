## Grammar notes

### Bangla numbers are individually irregular
Above 12, Bangla numbers do **not** follow a clean pattern like English "twenty-three". Each number 1–99 is its own word — `ekush` is 21, `baish` is 22, `tetrish` is 33, etc. Memorising all 99 is a separate project.

**For the wedding sprint:** learn 1–20 + the round tens (30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, 90) + 100 / 1000. That's about 25 numbers and covers ~90% of real situations (prices, ages, dates, quantities). Fill in the rest later, or fall back to English numbers in conversation — many Bangladeshis use English numbers for prices anyway.

### Hundreds and thousands
- `sho` = hundred. Prefix the multiplier: `ek-sho`, `dui-sho`, `panch-sho`.
- `hajaar` = thousand. Same pattern: `ek-hajaar`, `panch-hajaar`, `dosh-hajaar` (10,000).
- `lakh` = 100,000. South Asia uses lakhs instead of "hundred thousand". You probably won't need it for daily talk, but you'll hear it for big numbers.

### Asking and stating age
Age uses `boyosh` (age) + `koto` (how much):

- Question: `Apnar boyosh koto?` (formal) or `Tomar boyosh koto?` (familiar)
- Answer: `Amar boyosh [number] (bochhor)` — `bochhor` is optional but adds clarity.

Example: `Amar boyosh ponchash bochhor` = I am 50 years old.

### Asking and stating price
Two equivalent ways:

- `Eta koto?` = How much is this? (most common)
- `Eta-r daam koto?` = What is the price of this? (more formal / explicit)

Answer with the number + currency: `Eta ek-sho taka` (100 taka) or `Eta ponchash krone` (50 krone).

### "How many" with classifiers
For countable things, use `koy` + classifier:
- `koy-ta` = how many (things) — `Boi koy-ta?` = how many books?
- `koy-jon` = how many (people) — `Mehoman koy-jon?` = how many guests?

Answer with the number + same classifier: `dui-ta` (two things), `dui-jon` (two people).
