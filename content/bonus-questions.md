## Words
- ki = what (also: the yes/no question particle)
- kothay = where
- kobe = when
- ke = who
- keno = why
- kemon = how (state, condition)
- ki bhabe = how (manner, method)
- koto = how much
- koy-ta = how many (objects)
- koy-jon = how many (people)
- kar = whose
- kake = to whom / whom

## Grammar notes

### Two ways to make a yes/no question
1. **Add `ki` to a statement.** This is the most common — slip `ki` in (usually right after the subject):
   - `Apni khaben.` (You will eat.) → `Apni ki khaben?` (Will you eat?)
   - `Apni klanto.` (You are tired.) → `Apni ki klanto?` (Are you tired?)
   - `Cha khaben.` (You will have tea.) → `Cha ki khaben?` (Will you have tea?)

2. **Just rising intonation.** Like English "you went?" — same word order, voice goes up at the end. Works fine in casual speech.

### `ki` does double duty
The same word `ki` means both "what" *and* is the yes/no question particle. Position tells you which:
- `ki` near the start, no other question structure → yes/no marker: `Apni ki khaben?` = Will you eat?
- `ki` in the slot where the answer would go → "what": `Apni ki kheyechhen?` = What did you eat?

If unclear, just listen for whether the rest of the sentence has another question word.

### Question word position
Unlike English ("**Where** did you go?"), Bangla generally keeps the question word *where the answer would slot in*:
- `Apni kothay gechhilen?` = lit. "you where went" = Where did you go?
- `Eta ki?` = lit. "this what" = What is this?
- `Apnar nam ki?` = lit. "your name what" = What is your name?

This makes them sound calmer / less "front-loaded" than English questions.

### "How much" vs "how many"
- `koto` = how much (uncountable, prices, age) — `Eta koto?` (how much is this)
- `koy-ta` = how many (countable objects) — `Boi koy-ta?` (how many books)
- `koy-jon` = how many (people) — `Mehoman koy-jon?` (how many guests)

Same classifier system as numbers: `-ta` for things, `-jon` for people.
